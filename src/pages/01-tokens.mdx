## Character vs. word vs. subword

A transformer cannot read text — every string must become a sequence of numbers.

## Byte-pair encoding

Subword tokenization uses **byte-pair encoding (BPE)**. Starting from individual
characters, BPE repeatedly merges the most frequent adjacent pair into a new
token. Step through the algorithm:

<BPEMergesStepper client:load />

The result is a vocabulary of 100,000–200,000 entries. Common words stay whole;
rare words fall back to shorter pieces or raw bytes. The tokenizer is trained
separately from the model and frozen before model training begins.

## Token boundaries shift

The tokenizer is sensitive to casing, spacing, and punctuation. A single
character change can shift every boundary from that point onward. Toggle

## From text to model input

The model never sees your text — only a sequence of integer IDs wrapped in
structural markers. Type below to see how raw text becomes a token sequence:

<SpecialTokensWrap client:load />

<CacheCallout>

Tokenization is the transformer's **input interface**. Every downstream
operation — embedding lookup, attention, generation — operates on the token
sequence produced here. The vocabulary size sets the embedding table's row
count, and the sequence length determines how much work attention must do.
Nothing about the original text survives past this stage except the integer IDs.

</CacheCallout>
