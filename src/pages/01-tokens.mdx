## Text is not what the model sees

When you type a message, the model never reads individual characters. Before
any computation starts, the text is converted into **tokens** — chunks that
are larger than a single character but usually smaller than a full word.

Tokenization uses a technique called **byte-pair encoding (BPE)**, a form of
subword tokenization. The tokenizer learns which sequences of characters
appear together most often in training data and merges them into a single
token. Common words like "the" or "cat" end up as one token each. Rare or
long words get split into smaller pieces. This lets a fixed vocabulary of
tens of thousands of tokens cover virtually any text, including code,
URLs, and foreign-language content.

Notice how "characters" splits into two sub-word pieces. That is the
tokenizer deciding that "char" and "acters" are useful units on their own
(they appear in many other words), so it doesn't give "characters" its own
dedicated token.

## How casing, punctuation, and word length affect chunks

The number and shape of tokens changes depending on what text you feed in.
Switch between the examples below to see how:

<TokenChunks client:load />

Key observations:
- **Common words** (simple sentence) each get their own token.
- **Punctuation** is almost always a separate token — "It's" becomes `It` + `'s`.
- **Uppercase** matters: `Hello` and `hello` are different tokens with different IDs.
- **Long or rare words** are split into multiple sub-word pieces. "Untokenizable" doesn't exist as one token, so the tokenizer falls back to pieces it knows.

<CacheCallout>

Cache hits are checked **token by token**. The system compares the incoming
request's token sequence against the cached prefix — and the comparison is
exact. If a single character changes anywhere in the cached prefix, the
tokenizer may produce a different chunk boundary at that point or after it,
shifting all subsequent token IDs. The result: the token sequence no longer
matches, the cache entry is invalid, and the full prefix must be reprocessed
from scratch.

This is why small edits to early content (a typo fix in the system prompt,
a one-word tweak to a tool description) can silently blow the cache cold even
when the visible text looks almost the same.

</CacheCallout>
